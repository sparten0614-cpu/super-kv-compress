\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate KV cache compression across three orthogonal dimensions---quantization, eviction, and their interaction---measuring perplexity, retrieval accuracy (NIAH), and long-context task performance (LongBench).

\subsection{Setup}

\paragraph{Models.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (GQA 4:1, $n_\text{kv}=8$, $d=128$, 32 layers)
    \item Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 (GQA 4:1, $n_\text{kv}=8$, $d=128$, 32 layers)
    \item Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (GQA 7:1, $n_\text{kv}=4$, $d=128$, 28 layers)
    \item Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct (GQA 8:1, $n_\text{kv}=8$, $d=128$, 80 layers)
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Hardware.} Cloud RTX 5880 (48GB), Apple Mac Mini M4 (16GB).

\paragraph{Software.} llama.cpp (GGUF format), custom NIAH and eviction benchmark scripts.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item FP16 KV cache (no compression)
    \item q8\_0 KV (8-bit uniform quantization, 2x compression)
    \item q4\_0 KV (4-bit uniform quantization, 4x compression)
    \item TQKV\_6 (TurboQuant uniform 6-bit K+V, 2.67x compression)
    \item StreamingLLM (position-based eviction: keep sink + recent tokens)
    \item H2O (attention-score-based eviction: keep heavy hitters + recent)
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{PPL}: WikiText-2 perplexity (per-token, 16K context unless noted)
    \item \textbf{NIAH}: Needle-in-a-Haystack retrieval accuracy (5 positions: 10\%, 25\%, 50\%, 75\%, 90\%)
    \item \textbf{LongBench v2}: Multi-task long-context benchmark (20 questions, accuracy \%)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experiment 2: Qwen Failure Analysis --- K-Quantization as Root Cause}
\label{sec:exp-qwen}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrrl}
\toprule
\textbf{Qwen KV Config} & \textbf{PPL} & \textbf{$\Delta$PPL} & \textbf{Status} \\
\midrule
K=FP16, V=FP16 & 5.660 & --- & baseline \\
K=q8\_0, V=q8\_0 & 5.548 & $-$1.98\% & OK \\
K=FP16, V=q4\_0 & 5.577 & $-$1.47\% & OK \\
K=q8\_0, V=q4\_0 & 5.578 & $-$1.45\% & OK \\
\midrule
K=q4\_0, V=FP16 & 9694 & catastrophic & \textbf{K kills it} \\
K=q4\_0, V=q4\_0 & 6616 & catastrophic & \\
q4\_1 (K+V) & 8392 & catastrophic & \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Qwen2.5-7B asymmetric quantization diagnostic. V tolerates q4\_0 safely; K at q4\_0 causes catastrophic failure regardless of V precision. The optimal Qwen config is K=q8\_0, V=q4\_0 ($\sim$2.5x compression).}
\label{tab:qwen-diagnostic}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Key Finding 2: K-quantization is the root cause.}
K=q4\_0 with V=FP16 produces PPL 9694---confirming that \textbf{K-quantization alone} destroys Qwen at 4-bit. Two factors contribute:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{GQA ratio as vulnerability factor.} With $n_\text{kv}=4$, each KV head is shared by 7 query heads. K-quantization error enters the softmax as $\text{softmax}((Q + \delta Q)(K + \delta K)^T / \sqrt{d})$, where $\delta K$ is amplified by all 7 query heads simultaneously. Fewer KV heads means less redundancy and higher per-head information density.
    \item \textbf{Layer 0 outlier as trigger.} Qwen Layer 0 exhibits $K_\text{max}=93$ (extreme outlier), dominating the q4\_0 quantization range and destroying precision for normal-magnitude entries. Skipping Layer 0 from quantization reduces degradation from $+$104\% to $+$7.1\%.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Key Finding 2b: V is robust, K is fragile.}
The asymmetric diagnostic (Table~\ref{tab:qwen-diagnostic}) reveals a clean separation: V=q4\_0 with any K precision produces normal PPL (5.577--5.578), while K=q4\_0 with any V precision is catastrophic (6616--9694). On Llama-8B (GQA 4:1), where neither component causes catastrophic failure, we can quantify the sensitivity difference directly:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrrl}
\toprule
\textbf{Config (+ 50\% eviction)} & \textbf{$\rho$} & \textbf{PPL $\Delta$} & \textbf{Component} \\
\midrule
K=FP16, V=q4\_0 & 3.12x & +1.0\% & V quantization only \\
K=q4\_0, V=FP16 & 3.12x & +2.9\% & K quantization only \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{2}{l}{\textbf{Sensitivity ratio}} & \textbf{2.9$\times$} & K is 2.9$\times$ more sensitive \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{K vs V quantization sensitivity at matched compression. K-quantization costs 2.9$\times$ more PPL than V-quantization on Llama-8B, confirming that K errors are amplified by the softmax nonlinearity while V errors are linearly averaged.}
\label{tab:kv-sensitivity}
\end{table}

\noindent This is because K errors enter the softmax nonlinearity ($\text{softmax}(QK^T/\sqrt{d})$) where they distort the entire attention distribution, while V errors are merely linearly averaged. The optimal Qwen config is K=q8\_0, V=q4\_0 ($\sim$2.5$\times$ compression, PPL 5.578).

\paragraph{Mitigation: GQA-aware asymmetric quantization.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $n_\text{kv} \leq 4$: K $\geq$ 8-bit, V $\geq$ 4-bit (+ outlier layer skip)
    \item $n_\text{kv} \geq 8$: K $\geq$ 4-bit, V $\geq$ 4-bit safe across tested models
    \item General rule: K precision $\geq$ V precision, with the gap increasing as GQA ratio increases
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{eviction_gradient.pdf}
    \caption{StreamingLLM eviction gradient across models and context lengths. Cliff position shifts right with larger models and longer contexts. 70B shows no cliff up to 70\% (PPL improves).}
    \label{fig:eviction-gradient}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Key Finding 3: Eviction cliff scales with model size and context.}
All models exhibit a convex PPL curve with a ``cliff'' (Figure~\ref{fig:eviction-gradient}), but its position varies dramatically: Llama-8B at $\sim$67\% (16K), Mistral-7B at $\sim$85\% (16K), and Llama-70B shows \textbf{no cliff up to 70\% eviction at 16K} (PPL improves at both 50\% and 70\%). The same 70B model at 4K context shows +26\% at 50\% eviction---confirming that context length is the dominant factor, not model quality. The cliff shifts right with both longer context ($f(n) = 0.277 + 0.0405 \ln(n)$) and larger model scale. Notably, the 70B cliff appears at $\sim$85\% eviction (+3.12\% PPL), confirming that larger models tolerate significantly more aggressive eviction before quality degrades. At 32K context, the cliff exceeds 85\% (PPL +0.04\% at 85\% eviction)---far beyond the log-formula prediction of 70\%, suggesting that context redundancy grows super-logarithmically and the scaling formula underestimates eviction tolerance at long contexts.

\paragraph{Key Finding 4: PPL can \emph{improve} under moderate eviction.}
At 30--70\% eviction on 16K context, both Llama-8B and Llama-70B show PPL \emph{improvement} (up to $-$0.32\% for 70B at 50\%). This counterintuitive result suggests that evicted tokens add noise to the attention distribution; removing them is beneficial for average prediction quality. This is precisely why PPL is misleading for eviction---it rewards compression that harms retrieval (\S\ref{sec:exp-metric-gap}).

\label{tab:niah-matrix}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Key Finding 5: The fundamental causal limitation of eviction.}
Four distinct scoring strategies all fail at high eviction rates: StreamingLLM (position-based), H\textsubscript{2}O (attention-based), and Expected Attention with both single-layer and multi-layer K-statistics (attention-proxy). At 85\% eviction, StreamingLLM achieves 20\% NIAH, while both Expected Attention variants achieve 0\%. This is not a method-specific failure---it is a \textbf{fundamental causal limitation}. Eviction decisions are made during prefill (past), but the needle's importance depends on the future query. No retrospective scoring---whether based on accumulated attention, recency, frequency, or K-vector statistics---can identify tokens that will be important for \emph{unknown} future queries. K-statistics-based scoring (Expected Attention) performs even worse than position-based scoring (StreamingLLM) at 50\% eviction (40\% vs.\ 60\% NIAH). We attribute this to Expected Attention's global K-statistics failing to capture the importance of attention sink tokens---the initial tokens that StreamingLLM preserves by construction. This confirms that attention proxies based on K-vector statistics are strictly inferior to both actual attention scores and even simple position-based heuristics for eviction decisions.

\paragraph{Key Finding 6: PPL $\neq$ NIAH.}
At 50\% eviction, PPL \emph{improves} ($-$0.09\%) while NIAH drops to 60\%. This confirms that PPL and retrieval quality are \textbf{orthogonal dimensions}---optimizing for PPL can actively harm retrieval. This is the core of the ``metric gap'' (\S\ref{sec:exp-metric-gap}).

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{metric_gap_scatter.pdf}
    \caption{The metric gap: PPL vs NIAH under quantization (filled) and eviction (hollow). Quantization configs cluster at NIAH=100\% regardless of PPL change; eviction configs drop below the safety threshold despite neutral or improving PPL.}
    \label{fig:metric-gap}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Key Finding 7: PPL is an unreliable proxy for KV cache compression quality.}
Figure~\ref{fig:metric-gap} visualizes the fundamental disconnect. Three failure modes of PPL as a metric:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{False positive:} 50\% eviction improves PPL ($-$0.09\%) but destroys retrieval (NIAH 60\%). A researcher using only PPL would conclude eviction is free.
    \item \textbf{Insensitivity to architecture:} Qwen q4\_0 has PPL 6616 while Llama q4\_0 has PPL 6.348---a 1000x difference invisible without cross-model testing.
    \item \textbf{Orthogonality:} Quantization and eviction produce opposite PPL--NIAH signatures. Quantization: PPL degrades, NIAH holds. Eviction: PPL holds (or improves), NIAH degrades.
\end{enumerate}

We recommend \textbf{dual-metric reporting} (PPL + NIAH or equivalent retrieval task) as the minimum standard for evaluating KV cache compression methods.

\subsection{AutoResearch Phase 1: Complete Pareto Frontier}
\label{sec:exp-pareto}

We automated the search over 36 configurations (6 quantization $\times$ 4 eviction rates $\times$ 2 methods, minus invalid combinations) on Llama-3.1-8B at 4K context. 27 configs completed; 9 failed (all at 85\% eviction --- context too short). The Pareto-optimal configurations are:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrrl}
\toprule
\textbf{Config} & \textbf{$\rho$} & \textbf{PPL $\Delta$} & \textbf{NIAH} & \textbf{Region} \\
\midrule
q8\_0/q8\_0 & 1.88x & $-$0.08\% & 100\% & Lossless \\
q8\_0/q4\_0 & 2.46x & +0.07\% & 100\% & \textbf{Sweet spot} \\
q4\_0/q4\_0 & 3.56x & +3.13\% & 100\% & Moderate \\
\midrule
q8\_0/q4\_0 + 50\% evict & 4.92x & +0.97\% & 60\% & NIAH-limited \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Pareto frontier from 36-config automated search (Llama-3.1-8B, 4K context). A clear NIAH discontinuity separates the quantization-only region (NIAH=100\%) from the eviction region (NIAH=60\%). The ``sweet spot'' q8\_0/q4\_0 achieves 2.46$\times$ at +0.07\% PPL --- near-lossless asymmetric compression.}
\label{tab:pareto-frontier}
\end{table}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{pareto_frontier.pdf}
    \caption{Pareto frontier of compression ratio vs PPL change. Color encodes NIAH accuracy (green=100\%, orange=60\%). The q4/q4 transition point (orange ring) marks the boundary between NIAH-safe quantization-only configs and NIAH-limited eviction configs.}
    \label{fig:pareto}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Key Finding 8: The NIAH cliff has no middle ground.}
The Pareto frontier (Figure~\ref{fig:pareto}) reveals a binary NIAH transition: across all 27 valid configurations, NIAH is either 100\% (no eviction) or 60\% (any eviction at $\geq$50\%). There is no configuration achieving, e.g., 80\% NIAH --- the transition is binary. This further confirms the causal limitation: even modest eviction destroys retrieval for tokens in the evicted region.

\paragraph{Key Finding 9: Additivity enables Pareto prediction.}
All 27 joint (K,V) results are predictable from the 6 single-component measurements ($\delta_K$ and $\delta_V$ per quant type) within $\pm$0.1\% accuracy. This means the full Pareto frontier for \emph{any} new model can be predicted from $O(2n)$ calibration runs instead of $O(n^2)$ exhaustive search --- a critical efficiency gain for the AutoResearch pipeline (\S\ref{sec:conclusion}).
